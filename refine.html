Refine: Personal Statement

After a lot of hard work, you've come up with a rough draft of your personal statement for law school. Now, how do you improve it?

Maybe you can tell that there's something wrong with your essay, but you can't quite figure out what it is or how to solve it. Maybe you're feeling confident, but you still want to make sure you aren't missing any major problems. Maybe you just want to ensure that your personal statement is the best it can possibly be.

We can help!

Refine: Personal Statement is a self-guided online course that will take you through the most common problems in law school personal statements.

Throughout our seven-class series, consisting of 25 videos and corresponding exercises, we'll tackle everything from broad issues like on-target focus and good use of evidence right down to the most minute of grammar and spelling concerns.

As we proceed, we'll not only examine typical personal statement problems—we'll practice solving them through targeted exercises. Then it will be your turn to apply what you've learned to your own draft: examining it in-depth, discovering any flaws, and editing or rewriting to dramatically improve the final product. By the end of the course, you will have produced an impressive final draft, ready for submission to the law schools of your choice.

Refine: Personal Statement is designed to let you work at your own pace, so you can balance your day-to-day responsibilities with your law school application process. Spend as much or as little time as you need on your essay's specific problem areas. Course material will be available on demand until August 31st, 2014.

Why choose Refine: Personal Statement over traditional editing services?

By learning how to edit your own essay instead of handing it over to an editor, you get a few definite advantages. First, more and more law schools are asking that applicants disclose the use of editorial services in their applications. If you write and edit your own essay, however, you can safely and ethically claim that your personal statement is entirely your own work.

As well, by taking this class, you get the benefit of an entire targeted curriculum to peruse on your own schedule—instead of relying on an editor's potential availability. By doing your own editorial work, you stay in control of your time and your deadlines.

Is this class for me?

You should take this class if:

  • You've written a law school personal statement draft, but aren't sure whether your argument will be effective.
  • You're having a hard time transitioning from writing research papers to writing a convincing personal essay.
  • You aren't sure how to apply evidence well in the personal statement.
  • You aren't sure your essay fulfills what admissions committees want or shows them what they want to see.
  • You want to understand what works in an essay like this without relying on an editor.
  • You want to be able to tell all the law schools you're applying to that they produced your whole essay yourself, with no editorial assistance.
  • You have a great draft, but it's too long, and you aren't sure what to cut to meet the required length limits.
  • You've previously spent most of your editing time only proofreading, and you want to learn how to be a better editor overall.
  • You have a lot on your plate and need a course that works with your schedule.
  • You've done your research on the law job market, and are absolutely convinced that law is the career for you.
  • You're in your senior year of college or have already graduated.
  • You plan to apply to US or Canadian law schools.

You should not take this class if:

  • You have not written at least one complete rough draft of your personal statement. We'll be working on editing your essay, so you MUST have a draft in hand to complete the coursework. If you haven't completed a rough draft, but you want personal statement writing help, check out my other class, Launch: Personal Statement.
  • You have not yet completed your junior year of college. Concentrate on getting a great GPA for now!
  • You aren't sure that a law career is the best choice for you.
  • You haven't done any research about the current law market.
  • You plan to apply to law schools outside the US or Canada.
